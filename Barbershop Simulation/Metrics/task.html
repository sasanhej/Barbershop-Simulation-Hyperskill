Description

Now that we have learned how to simulate the work of a barbershop, let's calculate some metrics! In this project, we will focus on the metrics related to visitors waiting time:

  • the maximum waiting time: in our model, the visitor always waits to be served, but in real life, they may well leave if they realize that the waiting time will be quite long, so it is reasonable to understand how long the visitor may need to wait;
  • the closing delay: it's great that all customers who come before the closing are served, but delaying the closing for a few hours is not very cool, right?
  • the maximum number of people in line: nobody is going to wait forever, so if there are too many people, it will not be very convenient. If a client enters at the same time as another client gets served, then we will assume that the queue length has not increased. For example, when the first client arrived and immediately went to the barber, we assume that the line had zero length.

The first two metrics are fairly easy to calculate, so let's focus on the third one. When a new client arrived, he increases the length by \(1\), and it is decreased by \(1\), when a client gets served. Thus, if \(c_1, c_2, \dots, c_n\) is the time-ordered sequence of all additions and subtractions, then the length of the line by the time of the event \(c_i\) is equal to \(\sum_{j=1}^i c_j\), and we need to find such i that this sum is maximum.

Objectives

Write a program that takes arrival and service times as input data about, computes the maximum waiting time (in minutes), the closing delay (in minutes after 4 pm), and the maximum number of people in line and prints them in separate lines.

Input format

The first line contains the number barbers \(k\) and the number clients \(n\) today. In the next \(n\) lines there are pairs \(a_i\) and \(s_i\)separated by a space, where \(a_i\) and \(s_i\) are arrival time (in minutes, after 9 am) and service time (in minutes) of the \(i\)-th visitor.
